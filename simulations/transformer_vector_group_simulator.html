Clock Notation (Phase Shift)

The clock number is read exactly like a clock face. The primary (HV) line-to-neutral vector is always fixed at the 12 o'clock position and used as the reference (the "hour hand" that never moves). The secondary (LV) vector of the corresponding phase is drawn at the position given by the clock number, where each hour represents 30° of lag (clockwise rotation) of the secondary vector relative to the primary vector.

Why only certain clock numbers occur

A star↔star or delta↔delta transformation preserves phase, so Yy and Dd combinations can only land on even clock numbers (0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10) — in practice almost always 0 or 6, since these are obtained by simple lead reversal. A star↔delta or delta↔star transformation inherently introduces a ±30° shift on top of any winding reversal, so Dy / Yd combinations always land on an odd clock number (1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11) — in practice almost always 1, 5, or 11. This is a direct consequence of the 30° phase rotation that occurs whenever you convert a 3-wire delta quantity into a star (line-to-neutral) quantity, or vice-versa.

Parallel operation rule

Two transformers can only be paralleled directly if they belong to the same phase displacement group (i.e. same clock number, or a difference that can be corrected by relabelling terminals). Paralleling a Dyn11 with a Dyn1 (a 60° difference) without correction will drive large circulating currents and must never be attempted.
