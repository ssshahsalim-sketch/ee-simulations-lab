Key clarification: the VFD output voltage is genuinely pulsating — a train of fast PWM pulses switched at the carrier frequency. The VFD output current, however, is generally close to sinusoidal at the fundamental frequency, because the motor's leakage inductance naturally filters out most of the high-frequency switching content. Only a fairly small ripple at the carrier frequency normally remains superimposed on the fundamental current — that ripple shrinks further with a load reactor and becomes negligible with a sine wave filter.
Cable voltage drop is calculated from the Configurator tab's cable length and conductor size (line-to-line, 0.85 assumed motor power factor). On long, undersized runs this drop can noticeably reduce the voltage actually reaching the motor terminals relative to the VFD's own V/Hz output — watch for reduced torque and higher current draw on such runs, especially at low speed where the V/Hz output is already small.

VFD Sizing Rule of Thumb

Select a VFD whose continuous rated output current is at least equal to (and normally 100–125% of) the motor's full-load amps (FLA) from the nameplate — not just matching HP labels, since actual motor FLA can vary by design and efficiency class. Oversizing a VFD by more than one frame size is generally avoided because the drive's electronic overload protection is scaled to the VFD, not the motor, reducing protection accuracy, and because larger drives can have degraded low-speed current regulation for a small motor.

Reflected Wave & dV/dt Filter Selection

Fast IGBT switching edges (often <0.1μs rise time) launch a voltage wave down the motor cable. If the cable is electrically long relative to that rise time, the wave reflects at the motor's high-impedance terminals and can momentarily double the DC bus voltage at the motor — well beyond the drive's own DC bus rating. This "reflected wave" or "voltage doubling" effect stresses the first few turns of the motor winding insulation and accelerates bearing wear from induced shaft voltages. Longer cables and higher system voltages both increase the risk, which is why NEMA MG-1 Parts 30/31 style guidance ties the required protection level to both cable length and voltage class.

V/Hz Control Curve

Below base frequency (typically 50/60 Hz), the VFD holds output voltage proportional to frequency (constant V/Hz), which keeps magnetic flux — and therefore available torque — roughly constant: this is the constant-torque region. Above base frequency, voltage cannot exceed the drive's rated output, so V/Hz falls and available torque declines while power capability stays roughly constant: the constant-power (field-weakening) region. A frequently overlooked point: standard self-cooled (shaft-fan) motors lose cooling airflow at low speed, so continuous operation much below roughly 20–30% of base speed at full torque often needs a derating factor or an auxiliary blower — check the motor's inverter-duty data sheet.
