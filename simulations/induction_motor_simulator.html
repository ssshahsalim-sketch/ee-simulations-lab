Engineering Reference
How an Induction Motor Works
A 3-phase supply creates a rotating magnetic field in the stator at synchronous speed Ns = 120f/P. This rotating flux cuts the short-circuited rotor conductors, inducing an EMF and hence a current by Faraday's law. The interaction of this current with the stator flux produces a torque (Lenz's law), causing the rotor to follow the field — but always slightly slower. This speed difference is slip.
Ns = 120f / P  |  s = (Ns − Nr)/Ns  |  T ∝ sR₂/(R₂² + s²X₂²)
Torque-Speed Curve: The motor develops maximum torque (breakdown torque ≈ 2×rated) at a critical slip. Below that, the curve is nearly linear — the stable operating region. Beyond breakdown torque the motor stalls.
Variable Frequency Drive (VFD)
A VFD converts fixed AC to variable frequency/voltage, enabling precise speed control. The key principle: to maintain constant magnetic flux (and therefore constant torque capability), the V/f ratio must remain constant.
Φ ∝ V/f  →  keep V/f = const for constant torque capability
Below base speed (≤50Hz): Reduce V and f proportionally. Torque stays constant, flux stays constant.

Above base speed (field weakening): Voltage is clamped at rated, only frequency increases. Flux drops → torque capability drops as 1/f. Power stays roughly constant. Used in fans, machine tools.

Energy savings with VFD on variable torque loads (fans/pumps): Power ∝ N³, so reducing speed to 80% cuts power to 51%. Massive savings vs. damper/valve throttling.
Constant Torque Loads — T = Trated
Examples: conveyors, cranes, extruders, compressors (positive-displacement), hoists, reciprocating pumps.
Power is proportional to speed: P = T × ω. VFD energy savings are linear with speed reduction.

P ∝ N  (constant torque)
Variable Torque Loads — T ∝ N²
Examples: centrifugal fans, centrifugal pumps, blowers, compressors (centrifugal), agitators.
Power follows the affinity laws: P ∝ N³. Even small speed reductions give dramatic power savings.
P ∝ N³  (variable torque / affinity law)
Key Design Rule: The motor must be sized so its torque-speed curve lies entirely above the load torque-speed curve at all speeds, including transient conditions. An insufficient torque margin causes the motor to stall.
Motor Protection Considerations
Thermal overload: Current²×time determines heating. Slip above 8–10% increases rotor losses rapidly (P_rotor = s × P_air_gap). Prolonged operation at high slip causes overheating.

Voltage unbalance: Even 2% voltage unbalance can cause 6–10× increase in negative-sequence current heating. VFDs mitigate this.

Low voltage: Torque ∝ V². A 10% voltage drop reduces torque by 19%, raising slip and current. Risk of stall under heavy load.

VFD-specific issues: At low frequencies (<10Hz), cooling is reduced (if shaft-mounted fan). Use derate curves or separate ventilation. Harmonic currents from VFD switching can cause additional heating; use derating factor (typically 0.9–0.95 for PWM VFDs).
