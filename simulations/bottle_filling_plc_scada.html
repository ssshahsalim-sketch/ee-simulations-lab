Rungs scan top→bottom every 500 ms. COIL follows the rung live (multiple COIL rungs to the same tag act in parallel — OR logic, exactly like real ladder). SET/RESET latch a bit until reset/set elsewhere. TON (timer‑on‑delay) sets its output true once the condition has been true continuously for the preset seconds. CTU (count‑up) increments on each rising edge of the condition and sets its output true at the preset count; give it a Reset Tag to zero it (the example counter resets itself one scan after reaching preset — a common auto‑cycling pattern).
The process

Bottles are released from an infeed hopper onto a single conveyor, then travel through Filling → Capping → Vision Inspection → Reject → Packaging. It's modelled as an accumulation conveyor: one motor drives the whole belt, so the belt only moves while every station is idle — exactly like a small indexed filling line.

Your job
Write (or load) the ladder program in the Ladder tab. Sensors (photoeyes, level sensor, cap-ok sensor, fault bits) are read‑only inputs written by the simulated process. Coils (nozzle, valve, capper, pusher, conveyor motor…) are outputs only your program can drive. Everything downstream of the Start pushbutton depends on your logic — including whether the line actually stops on a fault.

Important: E‑Stop is hard‑wired
Pressing E‑STOP cuts the conveyor motor immediately regardless of what your program says — just like a real safety relay/category‑0 stop circuit, which by standard practice (ISO 13850 / ISO 13849‑1) is wired independently of the main PLC. Every other fault (jam, empty hopper, cap‑out, fill timeout) only stops the line if your CONV_RUN rung actually checks the ANY_FAULT bit — omit it, and the belt will run right through a fault. That's intentional: it's the same mistake that causes real incidents when interlocks are left out of a program.

A few real-world reference points
• Small craft filling lines run roughly 20–60 bottles/min; high-speed commercial rotary fillers exceed 1,000–2,000 bpm.
• Real sensors here map to actual hardware: through‑beam/capacitive photoelectric sensors for bottle presence, capacitive or radar/ultrasonic level sensors for fill height, and machine‑vision cameras for cap/fill inspection.
• TON and CTU are real IEC 61131‑3 ladder instructions (Allen‑Bradley calls them TON/CTU with .ACC/.CV and .DN members, which is exactly what the Tags tab shows here).
• Multi-step sequences like this are formally structured in industry under the ISA‑88 batch model (procedure → unit → operation → phase).
• The stack‑light and alarm colour conventions (green=run, amber=idle, red=fault) follow common ISA‑101 HMI / ISA‑18.2 alarm-management practice.
• Line KPIs are usually tracked as OEE = Availability × Performance × Quality — the packed/rejected counters here are effectively feeding the "Quality" term.
• Real machines also add a per‑station Hand/Off/Auto selector for maintenance jogging; it's left out here to keep the program logic front and center.
