The concept
V
ELCB — Earth Leakage Circuit BreakerVoltage-operated protection (older generation)

An ELCB is a voltage-operated device. Its coil sits between the installation's earth wire and a separate reference earth electrode driven into the ground some distance away. Under a fault, current flows from a live part into the earth wire and out through the earth electrode's resistance, which raises the electrode's voltage above true remote earth.

If that voltage rise crosses the device's trip setting — usually ~50 V — the relay operates and disconnects supply. Critically, the ELCB can only "see" fault current that returns to earth through the specific earth wire it monitors.

I
RCCB — Residual Current Circuit BreakerCurrent-operated protection (modern standard, aka RCD/ELCB-current-type)

An RCCB is a current-operated device. A toroidal current transformer encircles the live and neutral conductors together and continuously compares the current going out (L) with the current coming back (N).

In a healthy circuit these are exactly equal, so the net magnetic flux is zero. The moment any current leaks away — through a person's body, a damaged appliance, wet insulation, anything — an imbalance appears. If that imbalance exceeds the rated sensitivity (typically 30 mA for shock protection), the device trips within milliseconds — regardless of where the leaked current actually went.

This is why 30 mA was chosen internationally as the standard RCCB sensitivity for shock protection — it sits inside the AC-3 "let-go" band, well below the point where AC-4 fibrillation risk begins to climb, and a general-type RCCB clears it in well under 300 ms (usually far faster). A device that doesn't trip in this range — like an ELCB blind to the fault path — leaves a person exposed for as long as the fault persists.
