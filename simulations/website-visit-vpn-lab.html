Typing a web address looks like one action — it's actually nine. Step through the whole round trip your browser makes before a single pixel appears on screen.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEIt's like walking into a large office building to collect a specific file. You check if you already have a copy (cache), find which floor it's on (DNS), get buzzed in and verified at the front desk (TCP + TLS handshakes), hand over your written request (HTTP request), wait while staff prepare it (server processing), receive the folder (HTTP response), then sit down and arrange everything on your desk before you can actually read it (rendering).

A VPN inserts one extra stop in that same journey — a trusted relay that your traffic passes through before reaching the website, wearing a different "return address" the whole way.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEInstead of mailing a letter straight from your house, you drive to a trusted courier depot in another city, hand them your sealed letter, and they remail it under their own address. The recipient only ever learns the depot's address — never yours. Anyone watching the road near your house just sees you driving to the depot, not what's in the letter or where it's ultimately headed.

A VPN doesn't add encryption that an HTTPS site didn't already have end-to-end — your data was already locked when it left your browser. What it mainly changes is who can see your real address and which site you're contacting, and it shifts that visibility from your ISP to the VPN provider instead.

A VPN tunnel works by encapsulation — wrapping your already-addressed data inside a second, encrypted packet addressed only to the VPN server.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEPicture a sealed, addressed envelope (your normal encrypted request to a website) being placed inside a second, locked courier box marked only "Deliver to: VPN Depot." Anyone watching the courier van in transit can see it's going to the depot — nothing more. Only at the depot does someone unlock the outer box, read the inner envelope's real address, and forward it onward.

Common tunnel protocols, compared

General-purpose overview of how these protocols behave, not an endorsement of any specific provider. WireGuard has become the default for most VPN apps because its compact codebase is fast and easy to audit; OpenVPN remains useful specifically because it can run over the same port as ordinary HTTPS traffic, making it hard to block; IKEv2/IPsec is favored on phones because it survives switching between WiFi and mobile data without dropping the connection.
