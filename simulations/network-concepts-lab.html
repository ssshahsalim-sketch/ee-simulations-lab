An IP address is your device's street address on a network. It tells everyone else where to send data meant for you — and it can change depending on where you "live" right now.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEWhen you order something online, you give the courier your house address (192.168.1.10) — not a permanent ID, just wherever you happen to be receiving mail right now. Move to a new house (new WiFi, new café, new office) and you get a new address.

Private addresses only make sense inside your own building — the postman outside can't deliver to "Flat 4" without the building's real public address too. That's what NAT solves (see Sheet 7).

A MAC address is the serial number stamped into a device at the factory. Unlike an IP address (which changes with location), it's permanent — burned into the network card itself.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEIt's like the engine number stamped into your bike or car. The vehicle's "address" (where it's parked) changes daily, but the engine number stays the same for the machine's entire life, no matter who owns it or where it travels.

Both are needed for a delivery to succeed: the IP gets the data to the right building (network), and the MAC gets it to the right door on that floor (local device).

Subnetting divides one big network into smaller, organized sections. Instead of one giant building with no floor plan, you get clearly numbered floors, each holding a manageable set of rooms.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEA large apartment complex doesn't hand out addresses randomly — it's split into Floor 1, Floor 2, Floor 3, each with its own set of flat numbers. A subnet mask is just the rule that decides "how many floors" vs "how many flats per floor."

If an IP address is the building, a port number is the specific door inside it. The address gets a delivery to the right building — the port tells the building exactly which service should answer.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEYour office building has one street address but many doors: Reception, Accounts, Workshop. A visitor asking for "Accounts" doesn't need a different building, just the right door. Port 80 is the "web" door, port 22 is the "remote technician" door, and so on.

DHCP is how a device gets handed an IP address automatically the moment it joins a network — no manual setup needed.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEIt's exactly like checking into a hotel without a reservation. You arrive and ask if any room is free (Discover). The front desk checks and offers Room 192.168.1.45 (Offer). You accept (Request). The desk hands over the key and tells you how long your stay is valid for (Acknowledge / lease time).

DNS turns a name you can remember into the IP address computers actually need.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEYou call a friend by their name in your phone's contact list — you don't memorize their phone number. DNS is that contact list for the internet: type "google.com" and it quietly looks up the real number (IP address) for you.

NAT lets many private devices share one public address to reach the internet, while still routing replies back to the correct device.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEAn apartment building has one street address, but a dozen residents inside. When a resident calls out for a delivery, the building receptionist (router) notes who asked, then makes sure the delivery that comes back reaches the right flat — not a random door.

Sending data is like mailing a parcel — your message gets wrapped in layer after layer of packaging, and unwrapped the same way, layer by layer, at the other end.

DAY-TO-DAY EXAMPLEYou write a letter (your message), seal it in an envelope with a tracking slip (transport), put that in a box with the destination address (network), then hand the box to a courier who only looks at the outer label to decide which van it goes on (link). Nobody but the final recipient opens the actual letter.
