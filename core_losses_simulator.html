What Are Core Losses?

When an alternating magnetic field passes through a ferromagnetic core (iron, silicon steel, ferrite), two unavoidable energy loss mechanisms occur — Hysteresis Loss and Eddy Current Loss. Together they form Iron Loss (P_core) — a major factor in transformer and motor efficiency.

Hysteresis Loss

Cause: Magnetic domains in the iron must be realigned every half-cycle. This friction-like resistance to domain wall movement dissipates energy as heat.

Key insight: The area enclosed by the B-H loop equals the energy lost per cycle per unit volume. A wider loop = higher hysteresis loss.

Steinmetz Formula (1892):

P_h = K_h · f · B_maxn · V
K_h = Hysteresis coefficient (material dependent)
f = Frequency (Hz)  |  B_max = Peak flux density (T)
n = Steinmetz exponent (1.6–2.0, typically 1.6 for silicon steel)
V = Volume of core material (m³)

Effect of frequency: P_h ∝ f — doubles when frequency doubles.
Effect of flux density: P_h ∝ B_max^1.6 — highly sensitive to flux density.
Reduction: Use grain-oriented silicon steel, reduce B_max, use soft magnetic materials.

Eddy Current Loss

Cause: Changing flux induces voltages (Faraday's law) in the conducting core material. These induced voltages drive circulating currents — eddy currents — through the core resistance, dissipating energy as I²R heat.

Key insight: Laminating the core into thin sheets increases electrical resistance to eddy current paths, dramatically reducing losses.

P_e = K_e · f² · B_max² · t² · V / ρ
K_e = Eddy current coefficient
t = Lamination thickness (m) — CRITICAL parameter
ρ = Electrical resistivity of core material (Ω·m)
Note: P_e ∝ f² (quadratic) vs P_h ∝ f (linear)

Why laminations work: Cutting the core into N sheets reduces eddy current path area by 1/N², reducing P_e by 1/N². Typical 0.35 mm laminations vs 10 mm solid core = ~800× reduction in eddy current loss!

Total Core Loss (Steinmetz)
P_core = P_h + P_e
P_core = K_h·f·B_maxn + K_e·f²·B_max²·t²
At low frequency: hysteresis dominates
At high frequency: eddy currents dominate (grows faster)
Separation possible via Epstein frame testing per IEC 60404-2

Key Research Facts

Grain-Oriented Silicon Steel (GOES): Rolling aligns grains along the easy magnetisation direction (Goss texture), reducing hysteresis loss by 30–40% vs non-oriented. Used in large power transformers.

Amorphous Metals: No crystal structure means no domain walls to move → hysteresis loss ~75% lower than silicon steel. Used in distribution transformers where idle losses matter most.

Ferrite Cores: High resistivity (10⁶ × higher than silicon steel) makes eddy currents negligible — ideal for high-frequency applications (switching power supplies, RF transformers, >10kHz).

Nanocrystalline Materials: Ultra-fine grain structure gives best-of-both-worlds: low hysteresis (like amorphous) AND low eddy currents. Used in precision current transformers.

What is Stacking Factor?

Stacking Factor (k_s) is the ratio of the volume of actual iron to the gross (total) volume of the laminated core assembly.

When laminations are stacked, the insulating coating between sheets and surface irregularities mean the total stack height is greater than the sum of iron thicknesses alone.

k_s = V_iron / V_gross = t_iron / (t_iron + t_insulation)
Typical values: 0.90–0.97 for silicon steel laminations
Thicker laminations → higher k_s (less coating proportion)
Thinner laminations → lower k_s (more coating proportion)

Effect on Core Loss

Stacking factor affects core loss in two ways: Effective flux density increases if B_max is based on gross area (B_eff = B_gross / k_s), and effective iron volume = k_s × gross volume.

P_core_actual = P_core × k_s
B_eff = B_applied / k_s
Lower k_s → higher effective B_eff for same applied flux
→ increases P_h (exponential) more than P_e is reduced

Frequency Effect on Core Losses

This is perhaps the most critical design consideration. The two loss components scale differently with frequency:

• P_h ∝ f¹ — linear relationship (Hysteresis)
• P_e ∝ f² — quadratic relationship (Eddy current)

At low frequencies, hysteresis dominates. As frequency rises, eddy currents take over and grow much faster. This defines material selection for different applications.

Skin Effect & Flux Penetration

At high frequencies, induced eddy currents create their own opposing flux, causing the skin effect — actual flux penetrates only a shallow depth (skin depth δ) from the surface.

δ = √(ρ / (π · f · μ))
δ = skin depth (m)  |  ρ = resistivity (Ω·m)
μ = permeability (H/m)  |  f = frequency (Hz)
Lamination should be ≤ 2δ for full flux utilization
